Falling into a Schwarzschild black hole

The fall demo is a first-person version of the earlier black-hole renderer. The camera is no longer a fixed observer outside the hole: it is a massive particle following a timelike geodesic. Each animation frame advances that particle in its own proper time, builds a local relativistic camera frame around it, then ray-traces what light reaches that falling observer.

This page assumes the light-bending baseline and the Kerr-Schild integrator are already familiar. It focuses on the part those pages do not teach: how to make the camera itself a physical observer with clocks, conserved quantities, a local tetrad, and a horizon-crossing frame.

1 - The state of the falling observer

The simulation uses the Schwarzschild metric in units where the horizon radius is rs = 1 and c = 1:

ds² = -(1 - 1/r) dt² + dr²/(1 - 1/r) + r² dφ²

A falling body needs only a compact state: radius r, angle φ, proper time τ, distant Schwarzschild time t, plus two conserved quantities: energy E and angular momentum L.

The minimap lets you choose a local launch velocity as measured by a hovering observer at that radius. The code converts that local velocity into E and L:

γ = 1 / √(1 - β²),   E = γ√(1 - 1/r),   L = rγβφ 

const gamma = 1 / Math.sqrt(1 - beta2);
energy = gamma * Math.sqrt(1 - 1 / r);
angularMomentum = r * gamma * betaTangential;

2 - Proper-time integration

Once E and L are known, the worldline follows three first-order equations. These are what stepFall integrates with RK4:

dr/dτ = ±√[ E² - (1 - 1/r)(1 + L²/r²) ],   dφ/dτ = L/r²,   dt/dτ = E/(1 - 1/r)

The square-root term is the radial kinetic energy left after gravity and angular momentum have taken their share. The sign tracks whether the traveler is moving outward or inward; if an outward trajectory reaches a turning point, the sign flips and the fall begins.

3 - Reading the potential

The radial equation can be read as (dr/dτ)² = E² - V(r), with V(r) = (1 - 1/r)(1 + L²/r²). That makes it easy to preview whether a launch dives, turns around, or loiters near an unstable circular orbit.

4 - Two clocks disagree

The falling observer crosses the horizon in finite proper time. A distant Schwarzschild clock does not see the crossing finish, because dt/dτ = E/(1 - 1/r) diverges as r approaches 1. The demo reflects that: proper time keeps running, while the distant-time readout becomes unreachable after horizon crossing.

Why does the code stop distant time inside the horizon?

Schwarzschild t is a coordinate chosen by distant observers. It becomes singular at the horizon, even though the falling traveler feels nothing special there. Inside the horizon, decreasing r is as unavoidable as moving toward tomorrow, so the demo treats t as an outside-only readout and switches the visual ray tracer to horizon-safe coordinates.

5 - Building a first-person relativistic camera

A normal camera stores right, up, and forward vectors. A relativistic camera stores an orthonormal tetrad: one time axis and three spatial axes, all measured in the observer's local inertial frame. The code first computes the faller's local velocity β, then Lorentz-boosts the player's look basis by that velocity. 

photon = eTime + eRight * rdLocal.x + eUp * rdLocal.y + eForward * rdLocal.z;
rd = normalize(photon.yzw);

6 - Rays from a moving observer

The renderer traces photons backward from the falling camera, but the important new step is the launch frame. A screen ray first becomes a local photon through the tetrad above; only then does the shader integrate it through the black-hole spacetime. That separation is what lets the same sky renderer work for a hovering camera, a plunging camera, or eventually a freely falling Kerr observer.

local screen ray → tetrad photon → coordinate momentum → backward geodesic

The current Schwarzschild demo has a pragmatic split. Outside the horizon it uses the fast photon-potential method taught in the baseline tutorial. Inside the horizon it switches to a Hamiltonian in Kerr-Schild-like Cartesian coordinates, which do not blow up at r = 1:

H(q,p) = ½[-1 + |p|² - (1/r)(-1 + l·p)²]

The deeper research target removes this branch: use one Kerr-Schild Hamiltonian ray path everywhere, even outside the horizon, then use the Schwarzschild shortcut only as a test mode. The final color still needs the falling observer's motion, so the demo applies a compact gravitational/velocity shift: 

shift = gamma * (1 + dot(observerBeta, rayDir)) / sqrt(abs(1 - 1 / r));

7 - What the minimap is really doing

Dragging on the minimap is not a screen-space trick. It chooses a position, converts your drag direction into local radial and tangential velocity components, creates a new geodesic with launchFromLocal, and calls previewFall to draw a short proper-time future. Releasing the pointer simply lets that already-computed state start evolving in real time.

In the full Kerr version, this same UI action would create an observer tetrad and four-velocity in ingoing Cartesian Kerr-Schild coordinates, then validate the preview by watching energy, angular momentum, the null constraint for camera rays, and smoothness at the horizon.

8 - What this page leaves to the others

The fall page deliberately avoids reteaching photon rings, disk arcs, and Doppler beaming: those are covered in the baseline light-ray tutorial. It also avoids deriving the full Kerr Hamiltonian and shadow checks: those belong to the spin tutorial. The unique lesson here is the observer pipeline: choose a local launch, integrate the worldline in proper time, build the tetrad, and launch every rendered photon from that physical frame.
