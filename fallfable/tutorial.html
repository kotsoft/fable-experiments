Falling through the Kerr horizon

The fallfable demo puts the camera on a worldline that falls into a spinning black hole — and keeps the picture physically meaningful after it crosses the event horizon, all the way down to the inner horizon. The Schwarzschild and Kerr pages explain how to bend light around a black hole seen from a safe distance. This page covers what they don't: coordinates with no seam at the horizon, a camera that is a genuine relativistic observer, and the trick that makes an inside view possible at all — tracing light backwards in time.

Everything below uses geometric units (G = c = 1) with M = ½ and spin a = 0.425, i.e. a/M = 0.85 — the demo's parameters. All the formulas are exactly what src/fallfable/kerr.ts and the WGSL trace kernel run.

1 — Coordinates with no seam: Kerr–Schild

The textbook Boyer–Lindquist form of the Kerr metric breaks down at the horizon: metric components diverge there, and a naive integrator slows to a crawl and stops at the boundary. That is a fault of the coordinates, not the spacetime. The renderer instead uses ingoing Kerr–Schild coordinates, where the whole metric is flat space plus one rank-one correction:

gμν = ημν + f lμ lν,    f = 2M r³ / (r⁴ + a²z²)

lμ = ( 1, (r x + a y)/(r² + a²), (r y − a x)/(r² + a²), z/r )

Here (x, y, z) are ordinary Cartesian coordinates and r is an ellipsoidal radius defined implicitly by r⁴ − r²(ρ² − a²) − a²z² = 0 with ρ² = x²+y²+z². Every component is finite and smooth at the horizon — worldlines and light rays integrate straight through it without noticing. The price is that these coordinates encode the one-way nature of the horizon in a different way: the light cones tip over.

2 — The camera is an observer: geodesics and tetrads

The camera is a massive particle on a timelike geodesic. Both the camera (CPU, double precision) and the light rays (GPU) are integrated from the same super-Hamiltonian,

H = ½ gμν pμ pν,    dxμ/dλ = ∂H/∂pμ,    dpμ/dλ = −∂H/∂xμ

with H = −½ on the camera's worldline and H = 0 on light rays. Because the metric is stationary and axisymmetric, E = −pt and Lz = x py − y px are conserved — the test suite checks both to machine precision across thousands of integration steps.

A camera also needs a notion of "forward", "up" and "right" that makes local sense while moving at relativistic speed through curved spacetime. That is a tetrad: four vectors et, ex, ey, ez that are orthonormal in the metric, with et equal to the camera's four-velocity. The demo builds it by Gram–Schmidt in g from the desired look direction. A pixel's view ray is then assembled in that frame — which is why relativistic aberration and Doppler shifts come out automatically rather than being painted on.

One more frame matters: the planner's launches. "Start at rest with velocity β" has to be measured by someone. A static observer won't do — inside the ergosphere (r < 2M in the equatorial plane) standing still is impossible, as frame dragging forces every timelike worldline to co-rotate. The demo measures launches against the Eulerian observer of the Kerr–Schild time slicing,

uμ = ( √(1+f),   −f li / √(1+f) )

which is timelike at every radius — ergosphere, horizon, interior, everywhere. So the minimap lets you place yourself anywhere, including inside the hole.

3 — Rendering backwards: past-directed ray tracing

Here is the heart of the demo. The earlier renderers launch a ray from the camera along the view direction and integrate it forward in time, asking "where does this photon go?" Outside a static black hole that happens to give the right picture, because Schwarzschild looks the same with time reversed. But it fails twice for this demo:

First, Kerr is stationary but not static — running time backwards flips the spin. Forward-traced images of a spinning hole come out mirror-imaged, with frame dragging going the wrong way. Second, and fatally: inside the horizon every future-directed path ends at the singularity (section 1's tipped cones). Trace forward from an interior camera and every single pixel dies in the dark. The view would be black, which is wrong.

What a camera actually records is light that arrived. The photon reaching the camera from view direction n has momentum k = E(et − ni ei) in the camera frame. To find where it came from, integrate its time-reverse:

q = −k = −et + nx ex + ny ey + nz ez

a past-directed null ray, marched with the same Hamiltonian equations. Inside the horizon the future cone points entirely inward — so the past cone points entirely outward. Past-directed rays launched from an interior camera cross the horizon outward (smoothly, in these coordinates), find the disk and the stars, and the view stays alive until the geometry itself gives out.

The normalization is chosen so that q · ucamera = 1: every ray carries exactly one unit of observed energy. Then the red/blueshift of whatever the ray hits is a single dot product,

g = νobs / νem = 1 / (qμ uμemitter)

with the emitter's four-velocity — a circular-orbit frame for the disk, a static frame at infinity for the sky (where qt is conserved, so g = 1/qt with no extra work). Disk color shifts as a blackbody at g·T, and intensity is boosted by g³ — that one factor produces the bright approaching side and the dim receding side of the disk.

4 — Integrating well: analytic gradients and the shadow

Hamilton's equations need ∂H/∂x. The earlier renderers approximate it with central differences of H — six extra metric evaluations per derivative, and in 32-bit GPU floats the differences sit right at the precision floor. Because Kerr–Schild is so compact, this renderer differentiates the metric analytically instead. The only nontrivial piece is the gradient of the implicit radius:

∇r = ( r²x,  r²y,  (r²+a²)z ) / ( r·√((ρ²−a²)² + 4a²z²) )

from which ∇f and the Jacobian of lμ follow by the chain rule. The result is roughly six times fewer metric evaluations per step and an exact derivative — the CPU mirror of this code matches double-precision finite differences to five decimal places in the test suite, and conserved quantities hold to ~10⁻¹⁶ per step.

Step size adapts twice over. It scales with r, since curvature (and the disk's fine structure) live near the hole. And it scales with momentum: rays that skim the horizon have their coordinate momentum grow like eκλ — the numerical face of infinite redshift. The integrator shrinks the step in proportion, the ray freezes against the horizon, and once its momentum has grown by a factor of a few thousand the pixel is declared shadow and painted black. That is not a hack; an infinitely redshifted ray genuinely carries no light. The black hole's silhouette is exactly the set of pixels whose past never escapes.

Why a ray's momentum explodes at the horizon

Trace backwards from a pixel right at the shadow's edge and the ray asymptotes to the horizon, circling it ever closer without crossing. In ingoing coordinates the "outgoing" mode at the horizon scales like eκt, with κ the surface gravity — running the ray backwards winds t arbitrarily far into the past, so the components of pμ grow exponentially even though the path itself converges. Physically: for that photon to reach your eye with one unit of energy, it must have left with exponentially more. Follow it far enough back and the required emission energy diverges — nothing real could have produced it, so the pixel is black.

Matter that fell in ahead of you is visible too: the model keeps the disk glowing below the innermost stable orbit with a steep exponential fade, because real plasma doesn't switch off at the ISCO — it plunges, still radiating. From inside, that plunging stream is most of what there is to see of the disk.

The show ends at the inner horizon. A spinning hole has two:

r± = M ± √(M² − a²)   →   r₊ = 0.763,   r₋ = 0.237

Between r₊ and r₋ the radial direction is timelike and the fall is irreversible. But at r₋ the character changes again — and that surface, the Cauchy horizon, is where general relativity stops making promises. All the infalling light from the universe's entire future piles up there, blueshifted without bound; perturbation analysis says the surface is unstable and curls into a singularity of its own (the "mass inflation" instability). The renderer is honest about this: as the camera approaches r₋ the backward rays blueshift until the momentum guard trips, the window closes out, and the demo ends the worldline with the constraint watchdog rather than rendering fiction beyond it.

6 — The disk, briefly

The disk is an optically thin slab sampled along each ray: vertical Gaussian density with scale height 0.075r, temperature following a Novikov–Thorne-like profile T ∝ (rin/r)3/4(1 − √(rin/r))1/4, colored as a blackbody at g·T, beamed by g³, and attenuated by absorption so the near side has depth. Turbulent texture comes from value noise sampled in a frame that co-rotates at the local orbital rate Ω(r) — evaluated at each ray's own coordinate time, so differential rotation shears the pattern into trailing streaks and light-travel delay across the image is automatic. A per-pixel jitter on the march step keeps the slab from banding.

7 — Keeping it fast

The renderer never reads GPU results back. Each animation frame writes one small uniform buffer, dispatches the trace kernel into an HDR texture, and draws it to screen through an ACES tonemap — the CPU learns nothing about the pixels and never waits. Pacing comes from a two-frames-in-flight cap, and the trace resolution itself adapts to the measured GPU frame time, so the demo holds interactive rates on modest hardware and sharpens automatically when the view gets cheap. The analytic gradient of section 4 is what makes the per-step cost low enough for ~1200-step rays at interactive resolutions.
